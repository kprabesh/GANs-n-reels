[Google Brain] AdaGAN: A solution to the missing modes problem using a boosting meta-algorithm.

Ever wonder what would happen if you used AdaBoost for GANs? Me neither…, but Tolstikhin et al. did, and they did it, and it was awesome.

In this article we’re going to give a brief introduction to the problem attacked by the authors of the following paper: https://arxiv.org/abs/1701.02386, and maybe just scratch the surface of their methodology and approach. If you’re accustomed to reading ML conference papers, you’ll be used to the relatively short format. Most conferences have some sort of length limit, like NIPS for example which caps at 8 pages. This improves readability and helps everyone get to the point, especially since most of the novel methods published today either have the bulk of their lengthiness contained in the associated github repos, or are largely experimental and have results that can be summarized concisely. The papers published in journals, as well as those from the math and physics domains, are often much, much longer, and contain lengthy derivations and proofs that can occasionally stretch out into hundreds of pages. Although concision is always a goal in these fields, it’s often simply not feasible to do justice to the body of work in a low page count. The paper we’re attempting to cover in this article is a lot more like something you’d see in a math journal, as the authors first prove the feasibility of their approach by

“deriving a general result on the minimization of f-divergences over mixture models.”

We’ll attempt to briefly discuss the background and significance of their results, but we won’t even come close to giving it a thorough explanation in this short-form article.

So what’s the missing modes problem? Well, the authors give a wonderful introductory example having to do with the generation of pictures of animals which explains the issue and a possible approach:

“Imagine we have a large corpus, containing unlabeled pictures of animals, and our task is to build a generative probabilistic model of the data. We run a recently proposed algorithm and end up with a model which produces impressive pictures of cats and dogs, but not a single giraffe. A natural way to fix this would be to manually remove all cats and dogs from the training set and run the algorithm on the updated corpus. The algorithm would then have no choice but to produce new animals and, by iterating this process until there’s only giraffes left in the training set, we would arrive at a model generating giraffes (assuming sufficient sample size). At the end, we aggregate the models obtained by building a mixture model. Unfortunately, the described meta-algorithm requires manual work for removing certain pictures from the unlabeled training set at every iteration.”

So the missing modes problem is simply when a generative model is unable to produce examples in some region of the target space, in this case the giraffe region. Under the hood, what’s happened is that the generator network of the GAN has converged on one or a few of the modes (think categories of examples), but not all of them. If you’ve read my series on Progressive Growing of GANs, you might remember we briefly touched on the problem of variability v. quality with these networks: in order to get some variation in your generated data, you have to stray away from what the network thinks is “optimal”, otherwise you would just generate the same image over and over again. This is a generalization of that rather extreme example of the issue.

Now wouldn’t it be nice if there was some way to automatically build up a mixture model that treats adversarial examples within the data…?

Boosting.

The general AdaBoost algorithm is actually very simple. For a classification problem, there are bound to be examples in the data that get misclassified. What AdaBoost does is iteratively train several versions of the same model (it can be applied to almost any), modifying the data slightly each time to emphasize, that is, weight higher, the problem data points. The next model in the process focuses on the errors of the previous ones, and when we take a weighted (by confidence) majority vote, the end result is a mixture model which has fixed some of the “holes” in the original classifier. It’s very similar to what the authors described in the giraffe example, except AdaBoost does it all automatically through it’s clever re-weighting of the training examples. What the authors of this paper have done is apply a very similar boosting meta-algorithm to GANs, using the GAN as the initial model and using a rather complex re-weighting scheme to create a “new” version of the dataset.

The theory.

Section 2 of the Tolstikhin et al. paper serves mainly to prove a result about the minimization of f-divergences, which are functions which basically measure the “distance” between two probability distributions. Their aim is to prove that the algorithm they’ve designed is optimal and that

“if the GAN optimization at each step is perfect, the process converges to the true data distribution at exponential rate (or even in a finite number of steps, for which we provide a necessary and sufficient condition).”

Furthermore, they give a set of “weak learnability” conditions that ensure the process converges (exponentially) even when the optimization is not always perfect at each step.

But the connection between GAN optimization and minimization of f-divergences isn’t completely obvious. The GAN algorithm essentially optimizes a criterion over two functions, the generator and discriminator networks. Though it’s beyond the scope of this article, what it boils down to is that the minimization of any f-divergence can be solved by using an adversarial technique analogous to the GAN algorithm, and indeed we can see the similarities, since the training data and the generated data are simply samples from two distributions which we attempt to minimize the difference between by modifying the generator.

And so for this reason, the authors frame this part of the paper in the general context of f-divergences. Their results are deep and so go much farther with the specifics, but the mixture model aggregation that they’ve conceived turns out to converge very nicely and quickly.

Results.

Above we see AdaGAN applied to a toy example to demonstrate the missing modes problem. In the top left, we see our training examples in red, and the data generated by a vanilla GAN in blue. Notice it only covers one mode, so we’re missing three of them :( On the right we’re given a look under the hood of AdaGAN. The points are just the training data, but the colors display the re-weighting scheme going to work. Note it sees that we’ve missed those three modes, so it weights them higher (in red). Then the next iteration of the model treats the next mode, and so we on the bottom left newly generated points in blue covering another patch of the red training examples. And on the right we see the re-weighting scheme getting ready to treat the third one. In summary, this novel algorithm treats one of the main problems with training GANs. One question that may or may not be relevant is whether or not its possible to incorporate this “boosting” behavior into a single neural architecture so that the latent space representation will be smooth, a concern raised by the authors in their conclusion. I’m really not qualified to weigh in on this subject, but perhaps something with hidden layers that have unit edge weights until after a certain point in the training process?
